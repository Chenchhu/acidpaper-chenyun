A B S T R A C T

Carbonate reservoirs commonly feature rough-walled fractures with complex geometries and hydraulic properties, posing significant challenges in accurately evaluating the dissolution patterns and conductivities along these fractures during acid stimulation processes in oil, gas, and geothermal production. This study conducts an experimental investigation of acid treatment in carbonate fractures with varying roughness levels to analyze the transition patterns in the dissolution of these rough-walled fractures, particularly focusing on the effects of acid injection rate and fracture roughness. The results reveal that the dissolution patterns progressively change from uniform dissolution and channel dissolution to surface dissolution. This transition occurs either with an increase in the acid injection rate or a decrease in the fracture roughness. In response to these findings, a phase transition model has been developed to incorporate the dissolution pattern transition, taking into consideration the roughness of the fractures. The model is based on the degree of dissolution observed in the etching channels in the flow and longitudinal direction. Further analysis of fracture conductivities, considering different dissolution patterns, indicates that the formation of etching channels post-acidizing in these rough-walled fractures is the primary factor influencing changes in the conductivity of the fracture surfaces. A channel-dissolution coefficient λ is introduced for the evaluation of the dissolution pattern and fracture conductivity. The critical transition value from uniform to channel dissolution is identified as λuc ,and the transition from channel dissolution to surface dissolution is λcs . Analysis comparing fracture conductivities under different dissolution patterns reveals a preference for channel dissolution, which offers enhanced efficiency in acid treatment. For optimal treatment efficacy,it is advised that the channel-dissolution coefficient be maintained between λuc and λcs . These insights offer a comprehensive experimental and theoretical basis for the optimization of acid treatment techniques, aimed at enhancing the productivity of oil, gas, and geothermal operations in carbonate reservoirs with rough-walled fractures.

1. Introduction

Carbonate rock reservoirs, holding substantial reserves of oil, gas, and hydrothermal resources, hold significant potential for meeting the increasing global energy demands. 1−4 The technique of acid fracturing is widely employed to stimulate these carbonate reservoirs, thereby increasing energy production. 5,6,7,8 In these reservoirs,fractures characterized by complex geometries-such as roughness, tortuosity, and varying apertures-predominantly determine the hydraulic properties during production and significantly influence the efficacy of acid treatments. 9−11,12 Previous studies have carried out in-depth research on the response of mechanical properties of rocks in underground engineering under hydrochemical environment. For example, the superposition of chemical damage and thermal damage has a significant impact on the quality, wave velocity, porosity and compression failure characteristics of the sandstone. 13 Huang et al 14 analyzed the weakening law of acidity effect on the strength characteristics of roof hard rock, through the simulation experiment of hard limestone acidizing and characteristic parameter tests. However, quantitatively evaluating the conductivity of carbonate fractures dissolved in the process of acidizing is challenging. The mechanisms underlying acidizing and the dissolution patterns along rough-walled fractures in carbonate rocks under various injection conditions (including injection rate and acid concentrations) are yet to be thoroughly clarified.

Both experimental and analytical approaches have been proposed to quantify the morphology of acidizing and categorize dissolution patterns along carbonate fractures. Hanna et al. 15 investigated the impact of aperture variability on fracture dissolution growth, finding that fractures with a pronounced heterogeneity in aperture distribution exhibit relatively short breakthrough times, primarily due to the formation of preferential flow channels during acidizing. Dijk et al. 16 conducted an analysis of dissolution patterns in fractures, taking into account different acidizing parameters. They observed that at higher Damköhler numbers, dissolution patterns are predominantly influenced by the flow patterns and the fracture morphology, with buoyancy effects leading to preferential dissolution toward the upper wall of the fracture. Conversely, at lower Damköhler numbers, the dissolution patterns are mainly controlled by the mineralogical composition of the rock matrix. Elkhoury et al. 17 explored the interaction between geochemical dissolution and mechanical deformation on the acidizing morphology of carbonate fractures. Their findings revealed that acid-rock interactions lead to the formation of large-scale channels along the fracture surface at relatively low acid injection rates. Conversely, a more uniform dissolution pattern emerged at higher injection rates. However, it is crucial to note that the area of contacting asperities, determined by mechanical stresses and fracture surface roughness, restricts the spatial extent of dissolution. Building on this,Neumann et al. 18 conducted acidizing experiments on carbonate fractures and confirmed the presence of three primary dissolution patterns: uniform, channel, and rough. In uniform pattern the acid consumes the rock in a very homogeneous way and the main surface remains flat, and the channel patterns are characterized by the presence of a certain number of channels. The surface appears irregular, and the acidizing areas are either deep or thin and are considered to be rough patterns. Finally, Neumann introducing the concept of the tensile pattern, in which the surface is smoother than before acidizing. This led to the proposal of a novel empirical conductivity model specifically for acidized fractures exhibiting the tensile pattern. Additionally, Lu et al. (2017) introduced four innovative parameters: cross-section profile length, longitudinal section profile length, as well as horizontal tortuosity ratio and longitudinal tortuosity ratio to delineate the structure of the rough surface. Utilizing digital techniques, they categorized the acidized fracture surfaces into nine distinct etching patterns. Furthermore,Wang et al. 19 investigated the transitions of dissolution patterns along radial rough-walled fractures, developing analytical models to quantitatively assess these patterns in a phase diagram characterized by varying Péclet and Damköhler numbers. Nevertheless, the initial roughness along fractures, which significantly influences fluid flow behavior and etching paths, was not factored into the quantitative evaluation of dissolution patterns during acid treatment with different acidizing parameters. This oversight highlights a gap in systematic quantitative research on fracture dissolution patterns and their corresponding conductivities in the context of acid treatment.
Different dissolution patterns emerging from the acid-rock reaction significantly influence fracture conductivity, a critical factor in geo-energy production within carbonate reservoirs. Extensive research has been dedicated to evaluating fracture conductivity, particularly focusing on the heterogeneous interactions between acid and rock during acid treatment processes. Gong et al. 20 conducted measurements of fracture surface roughness and conductivity following acidizing experiments. Their findings underscored that the geometrical roughness of fracture walls and the inherent strength of the rock are the primary determinants of the conductivity of acidized fractures. Notably, prolonged acid contact leads to increased surface roughness and, subsequently, higher hydraulic conductivity. Acidizing reduces the compressive strength of rocks, which in turn causes uneven surfaces to deform more rapidly under stress. Deep etching channels, typically formed by injecting acid at specific concentrations, contribute to relatively high hydraulic conductivity,even under significant closing stress along the fracture. 21 Asadollahpour et al. 22 investigated the impact of varying acidizing durations on the evolution of dissolution morphology and the conductivity of rough-walled fractures. Their results indicated that prolonged acid injection led to greater normal deformation of the fracture, resulting in reduced final conductivity. Experimental data also demonstrated that the conductivity of an acidized fracture increased with higher acid injection rates under conditions of low confining stress. 23 However,under relatively high confining stress scenarios, the conductivity of the acidized fracture exhibited a slight decrease with increased acid injection rates. The study by Mehrjoo et al. 6 highlighted that higher fracture conductivity in calcite was attainable by elevating the acid concentration, whereas the reverse trend was observed in dolomite. The type of acid used substantially affects the resulting hydraulic properties of fractures, with different rock strengths yielding varied outcomes. In most instances, emulsified acid was found to achieve superior ultimate fracture conductivity. Nino et al. 24 conducted acidizing experiments on fractures with diverse surface geometries across various rock types. Their research highlighted that the mechanical behavior of rough-walled dolomite fractures significantly influenced both the dissolution patterns and hydraulic conductivity following acid treatment. While prior studies have acknowledged the considerable impact of the uneven acidizing morphology of fractures on post-acidizing hydraulic properties, there remains a notable gap in systematic quantitative investigations. This particularly pertains to the combined effects of initial roughness and acid flow rate on the dissolution patterns in acidized fractures. Consequently, the relationship between the etching morphology and the resulting fracture conductivity is yet to be comprehensively understood.
This present study experimentally investigates the dissolution patterns and conductivity of carbonate fractures with defined rough-walled characteristics by performing fluid flow tests and acidizing tests. Three distinct dissolution patterns (uniform dissolution, channel dissolution, and surface dissolution) are identified, taking into account variations in fracture roughness and acid injection rates. Additionally, a phase transition model is developed to examine the transitions in these dissolution patterns, with a particular focus on fracture roughness. This model is instrumental in quantifying the etching phase diagram based on the extent of dissolution within the etching channel in both the flow and longitudinal directions. Following this, a channel-dissolution coefficient is defined to evaluate the dissolution pattern and the fracture conductivity. An in-depth investigation into the acidizing efficiency allows for the quantitative determination of the preferred dissolution pattern. The findings from this study are expected to offer a robust experimental and theoretical foundation for enhancing acid treatment techniques, with the aim of improving the productivity of oil, gas, and geothermal extraction in carbonate reservoirs.

2. Materials and methods

The study uses a fluid flow testing system to conduct acidizing experiments and assess water flow pre- and post-acid treatment (Fig. 1a). This system is comprised of components such as an advection pump, a core holder, and an electronic balance. In order to reduce the influence of gravitational forces on fluid flow, samples were oriented horizontally within the core holder. Rough-walled fractures were generated through a Brazilian splitting test applied to 25 cylindrical limestone samples, each measuring 25mm×50mm ,sourced from Yichang,Hubei.
The mineral composition and pore structure characteristics of the collected limestone is analyzed by X-ray diffraction and mercury injection test, as shown in Tables 1 and 2. For acid flow confinement within the fractures,an ETFE corrosion-resistant gasket of 0.05mm thickness was integrated into the split sample's side seams and sealed. After the sealant had sufficiently dried, the assembly was wrapped with water-stop tape and heat-shrink tubing, followed by a 24-h saturation in distilled water to ensure homogeneous single-phase flow along the fracture for the duration of the experiments. High-density, three-dimensional point cloud data of the fracture surface were acquired using a 3D laser scanner (TriScan 500) with a precision of 0.005mm ,enabling the generation of an elevation contour map (Fig. 1b). The roughness coefficient (JRC) was employed to quantify the uneven undulation of the fracture surface, 25 with samples categorized into five roughness groups (3-7, 7-10, 10-13, 13-15, 15-19), each group containing five samples.

In total, 25 limestone fracture samples underwent acidizing and fluid flow testing to determine changes in acid dissolution. Prior to dissolution, fluid flow was assessed at different injection rates to obtain pressure differences between the inlet and the outlet, recording the resultant pressure gradients ∇P to calculate the conductivity of different rough fractures before acidizing. In the acidizing experiment,a 10% solution of hydrochloric acid was administered to five distinct roughness groups of samples at specified acid injection rates v(0.5,1,2,3,4mL/min) over a 20-min period, as detailed in Table 3. Throughout the experimentation phase, a constant confining pressure of 2.5 MPa was maintained, and the fluid temperature was kept stable (Fig. 1c). The pressure difference at each end of the fracture sample was recorded at every acidizing interval. The interaction between the acid and the rock modified the fracture's geometry, thereby altering its hydraulic characteristics. Once a dominant etching channel was formed from the inlet to the outlet of the fracture, the pressure difference between the two ends decreased to a negligible value, approaching zero. Following acid treatment, the test fluid was replaced with water to perform another set of fluid flow tests, which determined the post-acidizing conductivity of the fracture surfaces. Additionally, the 3D scanner was employed again to capture the changed morphology of the fracture surfaces following acidization.

3. Results and discussion

3.1. Characterization of acidizing fracture morphology and dissolution patterns

The acidizing morphology of rough-wall fracture surfaces is effectively illustrated through contour maps that demonstrate the variation in dissolution height (Δb) along the fracture surface. Fig. 2 presents contour maps depicting these dissolution heights under various acid injection rates (Pe) and fracture roughness coefficients arranged in a phase diagram. Analysis using the Pe−JRC phase diagram reveals that under similar roughness conditions, slower acid flow rates into the rock fractures prolong the contact time between the acid and the limestone at the entrance end. Consequently,hydrogen ions (H+) are predominantly consumed at this entrance, preventing the formation of an etching channel within the 20-min timeframe. This results in a uniform dissolution pattern across the fracture surface (e.g., samples A1, A2, and A3). Conversely, higher acid injection rates facilitate quicker acid penetration, accelerating the acid-rock reaction. Over time, this leads to a successful breakthrough across the fracture surface and the formation of an etching channel traversing the entire fracture. Once the etching channel is formed, continued acidizing fosters the expansion of this channel, resulting in surface dissolution patterns (e.g., samples E1 and E2). When examining the impact of roughness at a consistent injection rate, it is observed that lower roughness levels lead to a more uniform distribution of fracture surface openings. This increases the contact area between the acid and the rock, resulting in a surface dissolution pattern post-acidizing (e.g., samples E4 and E5). On the other hand, as the undulation degree of the fracture surface intensifies, the increased roughness diminishes the contact area for the acid-rock reaction and restricts the fluid flow within the fracture. This leads to a diminished development of the etching channel, an outcome known as channel dissolution, which is exemplified by samples C1 and D1.
The acidizing morphology fractal dimension Dv and the acid breakthrough time T serve as critical metrics in the characterization of acidizing morphology. The fractal dimension of the acidized morphology, calculated using the box-counting method applied to binary images, distinguishes between areas of varying acidization degrees. 26 Within these binary images, the white and black regions denote areas of lesser and greater acid effects, respectively. The fractal dimension, as depicted in Fig. 3a, quantifies the spatial distribution of the acidized regions on the rough-walled fracture surface, providing an accurate assessment of the etching channels and fracture surface geometries. As shown in Fig. 3b, different dissolution patterns are distinguished by color, and different symbols in the same color represent acid injection rates. Areas exhibiting uniform dissolution on the Q−Dv plane correspond to a fractal dimension Dv below 1.47,whereas a Dv above 1.65 signifies surface dissolution patterns. On the other hand, acid flowing into fractures will push forward after enlarging the pore size due to the rapid and sufficient acid-rock reaction. This progression is marked by the formation of an acidized pathway across the fracture surface, which is evidenced by a sharp decline in the pressure between the inlet and outlet. This pivotal moment is recorded as the acid breakthrough time T (Fig. 4a). Within the Q−T phase space,uniform dissolution is characterized by T values exceeding 20min ,and surface dissolution is indicated by T values less than 7min ,with times in between indicative of channel dissolution patterns (Fig. 4b). The integration of the fractal dimension and acid breakthrough time provides a comprehensive framework for validating the dissolution patterns observed throughout the acidization experiments.
Subsequent to delineating dissolution patterns, the influence of roughness and injection rate on their transformation was examined. For a lesser degree of roughness (JRC=3−7) ,a critical flow rate Qc of 1 mL/min marks the transition from uniform to channel dissolution. Furthermore,at Qc of 2mL/min ,a progression from channel to surface dissolution was observed. Notably, Qc is not a static value; it varies in accordance with the fracture's roughness. With a rise in JRC, the threshold Qc for transitioning from uniform to channel dissolution rises from 1 to 2mL/min ,while the Qc required for a change from channel to surface dissolution escalates from 2 to 4mL/min . Consequently,it is evident that an increase in roughness JRC necessitates a higher acid injection rate Qc to facilitate the formation of etching channels. This phenomenon is crucial for analyzing fluid behavior within acidized fractures. The empirical observations of acidizing pathway formation across varying degrees of roughness provide substantial insights, significantly contributing to both the practical application and theoretical understanding of acidizing in rough-walled fractures.

The total volume of acid required to initiate breakthrough, denoted as PVtot  ,is a critical measure for assessing the efficiency of acid treatment in forming dominant etching channels. PVtot  represents the minimum volume of acid required to create an etching channel at a given acid injection rate, signifying the point of acid breakthrough. As illustrated in Fig. 5,the relationship between PVtot  and fracture roughness is explored across different dissolution patterns, specifically channel and surface dissolution, under varying injection rates. Notably, uniform dissolution is excluded from this analysis due to the inherent difficulty in forming an etching channel within the designated 20-min injection period. The data reveal that PVtot  escalates with an increase in fracture roughness, regardless of the acid injection rate. This trend is attributed to the more intricate micro-concave-convex structures and irregularities present on rougher fracture surfaces, which necessitate greater volumes of acid to effectively penetrate these barriers. Additionally, heightened surface roughness results in more complex flow paths and, consequently, longer actual flow distances, further increasing the acid volume required for etching channel formation. Moreover, when comparing dissolution patterns,it is observed that the acid volume PVtot for channel dissolution is consistently lower than that for surface dissolution in fractures of comparable roughness. This discrepancy becomes more pronounced with increasing fracture roughness, suggesting that channel dissolution patterns are more efficient in acidizing,requiring a lower PVtot to breakthrough time with different acid injection rates. 3.2. Theoretical model for dissolution pattern transitions

This section quantitatively investigates the impact of fracture roughness and acid injection rate on the emergence of various acidizing morphologies along the fracture surface. The experimental analysis underscores that the development of dissolution patterns is related to the width and depth of the etching channel in rough-walled fractures after acidization. Prior research has represented the geometry of the etching channel in porous media under a linear flow assumption using two parameters (lx and ly) ,where lx is the penetration length of the etching channel along the flow direction,and ly is the penetration length transverse to the flow direction. 27 In the context of acid treatment in rough-walled fractures, the convective effect across the fluid flow path is minimal. As depicted in Fig. 6,the acidizing penetration length (ly) of the etching channel, perpendicular to the acid flow direction, is quantified by the following formula, derived from the studies of Lichtner et al., 28 Steefel and Lasaga, 29 and Szymczak and Ladd 30 :
(1)ly=(Dte0keff)12
where keff  is the effective reaction rate constant with a value of 0.0729,31 e0 is the initial fracture aperture that depends on the gasket thickness (e0 =0.05mm ),and Dt represents the lateral dispersion coefficient. This coefficient can be estimated using the empirical formula Dt=(1+ λtPe)D ,where λt=0.1.32 Conway et al. 33 proposed that the modified expression of the diffusion coefficient D in the HCl-limestone system is estimated to be approximately 3×10−9m2/s .
Furthermore,Szymczak et al. 30 parameterized the boundary curve of the etching channel, proposing that its shape is dependent on the interaction between diffusive transport normal to the flow and convective transport along the flow direction. 3.3. Evaluation of conductivity in rough-walled fractures pre- and post- acidization

The investigation into the conductivity variations pre- and post-acidization was carried out by analyzing fluid flow test outcomes and the discernible patterns of fracture surface dissolution. As shown in Fig. 8, it is apparent that the predominant factor influencing fracture surface conductivity prior to acidization is the alteration in roughness. Observations indicate a progressive decline in conductivity, from a range of 10−706μm2∙cm ,as the JRC gradually increases from 4 to 18 . Notably, the decrease in conductivity becomes markedly less pronounced when the roughness exceeds a JRC of 12. This trend is attributed to the increased undulations on the fracture surface as roughness escalates, which introduces increased resistance to the fluid flow within the fracture, thereby diminishing the effective flow area.
The creation of etching channels across the fracture surface emerges as the principal determinant of conductivity following acidization, as depicted in Fig. 8b. Post-acidization, the fracture surface conductivity spans from 46 to 48,000μm2∙cm ,presenting a maximal variance of three orders of magnitude. When correlating this with the Pe−JRC phase diagram, it is discerned that under conditions of lower flow rates (0.5 and 1mL/min ) coupled with higher roughness (JRC =13−19 ), the post-acidization fracture surface conductivity experiences negligible variation due to the absence of etching channel formation. Conversely, with an increase in acid injection rates and a reduction in roughness, etching channels initiate formation along the fracture surface, leading to a substantial surge in conductivity changes. This is attributable to the primary flow channel's increase in the fracture surface opening, altering local flow patterns within the fracture. Consequently, the fluid can traverse directly through the etching channel without encountering the frictional resistance on the fracture surface. At further increased acid injection rates ( 3 and 4mL/min ),the expansion of etching channels continues both vertically and in-depth. However, it is noteworthy that subsequent alterations in fracture conductivity are comparatively minor. Thus, the advent of an etching channel standardizes fracture conductivity across varying dissolution patterns. Despite the disparities in roughness and acid injection rates fostering diverse dissolution patterns such as channel and surface dissolution and influencing local fluid resistance, the overall impact on fracture fluid flow remains relatively insubstantial owing to the limited scale of rock samples and the low fluid flow rates employed in post-acidization testing. Field applications substantiate the efficacy of acidizing stimulation techniques in increasing the yield of oil, gas, and geothermal resources from carbonate reservoirs. 34 However,during on-site construction,the efficiency of acid treatment also needs to be considered. Distinct fractures may exhibit similar conductivities but differ in acid consumption depending on variables such as fracture roughness, the rate of acid injection, and the duration of the acidizing procedure. Therefore, optimizing the parameters of the acidizing operation necessitates an evaluation of both the conductivity and the acid treatment efficiency to ensure a balance between the optimal use of resources and the effectiveness of reservoir stimulation. When conductivity is analyzed in conjunction with the channel-dissolution coefficient λ and the total volume of acid injected (PVtot ) ,it becomes apparent that fractures subject to uniform dissolution (λ<λuc) demonstrate negligible differences in conductivity before and after acidizing, indicative of less effective acid treatment. Conversely, fractures undergoing channel dissolution (λ>λcs) show a pronounced response to the acidizing process. Nevertheless, while the transition from channel to surface dissolution does not markedly affect the conductivity of rough-walled fractures, it results in a considerable increase in PVtot  . Consequently,to ensure a significant acid treatment effect and practical acid usage during on-site acid injection operations, it is advisable to maintain the operational parameters within an optimal range (λuc<λ<λcs) ,corresponding to the channel dissolution pattern.

4. Conclusion

The current study conducts a systematic examination of dissolution patterns and the subsequent changes in conductivity resulting from variations in acid injection rates and fracture roughness during the acidization of rough-walled fractures. A phase transition model is developed to predict the transition between different dissolution patterns, taking into account fracture roughness. This model quantifies the etching phase diagram by analyzing the degree of dissolution within the etching channel, both along the direction of flow and longitudinally. Key findings are summarized as follows:
Within the scope of the acidizing experiments on fractures, we identified three predominant dissolution patterns: surface dissolution, channel dissolution, and uniform dissolution. It is evident that an increase in acid injection rate or a decrease in fracture roughness initiates a progression from uniform dissolution to channel dissolution, resulting in surface dissolution. Notably, fractures characterized by elevated JRC necessitate substantially higher acid injection rates to effectuate a change in dissolution patterns.
We propose a phase transition model that integrates fracture roughness and acid injection rate to accurately predict the shifts between dissolution patterns. This model exhibits strong agreement with empirical findings. A channel-dissolution coefficient λ is defined to evaluate the dissolution pattern. Observations indicate that with an increase in the acid injection rate (Q) ,there is a corresponding augmentation in the dissolution of the etching channel, both in terms of width and depth,which,in turn,increases the λ value. The threshold for the shift from uniform to channel dissolution is established at λuc(λ=1.37 ×10−6 ),while the transition to surface dissolution is initiated at λcs(λ= 1.72×10−6 ).
An analysis of fracture conductivities considering different dissolution patterns indicates that the formation of etching channels after acidizing is critical in altering the conductivity of the fracture surface. In instances of uniform dissolution where etching channels are not established, the post-acidization conductivity of the fracture surface exhibits minimal variance from its pre-acidization state. Conversely, with the initiation of channel and surface dissolution processes, the formation of etching channels leads to an expanded fracture surface aperture, significantly enhancing the conductive properties of the fracture surface. Comparative analysis between the fracture conductivities and the total acid injection volume PVtot  across distinct dissolution patterns suggests a preference for channel dissolution due to its superior acid treatment efficacy. It is recommended that the channel-dissolution coefficient be maintained within the targeted range of λuc and λcs to achieve this high efficiency.
Future research is essential to further explore optimal acid injection parameters, particularly under varying Damköhler number conditions. This will aid in selecting the most effective dissolution patterns and enhancing the stimulation efficiency in acidizing applications.
